# Whale Tracking

Whale Tracking gives you visibility into what the biggest players in the market are doing. Large traders — "whales" — often move ahead of significant price action. By monitoring their activity, you can position yourself alongside smart money.

## Features

### Whale Alerts

Real-time notifications when unusually large transactions occur. These alerts capture big position openings, closings, and transfers that can signal upcoming volatility.

### Whale Trades

A historical log of large trades, showing:

- The coin and exchange involved
- Size of the transaction
- Direction (buy or sell)
- Timestamp
- Associated price at the time of trade

Use this to see patterns — if whales have been consistently buying a coin over several days, it may indicate accumulation ahead of a move.

### Recent Trades

A live feed of the most recent large transactions across all tracked exchanges. This gives you an up-to-the-minute view of whale activity without waiting for alerts.

### Top Traders

A leaderboard of the most successful large traders, ranked by performance metrics. See who's been making the best calls and which coins they're active in.

### Coin Summary

Per-coin breakdown of whale activity, showing:

- Total whale transaction volume
- Net direction (more buying or selling)
- Number of unique whale addresses active
- Historical whale activity trends

## How to Access

Navigate to **Trades** in the sidebar of the web app. From there, you can switch between the Whale Alerts, Whale Trades, Recent Trades, Top Traders, and Coin Summary tabs.

## How to Use Whale Data

**Confirmation signal** — If you see a strong signal on the Scanner and whale activity confirms the direction, it adds conviction to the trade.

**Contrarian signal** — When whales are doing the opposite of retail (e.g., buying while the market is panicking), it may indicate a reversal is coming.

**Timing** — Whale activity often picks up 30–60 minutes before major moves. Watching real-time whale alerts can give you an early heads-up.

**Context matters** — A single large trade might be a hedge, not a directional bet. Look for patterns of consistent activity in one direction over time, not isolated events.
